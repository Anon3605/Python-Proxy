    <h1 id="simple-sites">The Unreasonable Effectiveness of Simple Websites</h1>
    <p>
      Posted on Sun 11 February 2024 in <a href="https://fd93.me/category/general.html">general</a>

    </p>
  </header>


  <div>
    <p><img alt="" src="/images/pexels-tirachard-kumtanom-587441.jpg"></p>
<p>Simple websites are better than beautiful ones.</p>
<p>I'll repeat that for the folks in the back.</p>
<p><strong>Simple websites are better than beautiful ones.</strong></p>
<p>Let me be clear what I mean by a simple website. I don't mean a Wordpress site
with a minimalist theme. I also don't mean a Substack, a Medium, or a Blogger
site (remember those?).</p>
<p>No, what I mean is an old-fashioned Web 1.0 website. For style examples, 
take <a href="https://paulgraham.com/">Paul Graham's site</a>,
<a href="https://news.ycombinator.com/">Hacker News</a>, 
<a href="https://madhadron.com/programming/index.html">madhadron's blog</a>, and <strong>this
site</strong>. (To my lasting regret I have not achieved the spare minimalism of pg's
blog.)</p>
<p>These sites have two things in common:</p>
<ul>
<li>They look like they're from the 90s. They suck.</li>
<li>They're created by experienced developers.</li>
</ul>
<p>Paul Graham, being one of the most successful investors in the Valley, easily
has the resources to have a <a href="https://www.apple.com/">modern site</a> with animations,
beautiful photography, and fancy scrolling-in pieces of text.</p>
<p>So why doesn't he?</p>
<p>Modern frameworks like React make it very easy to create aesthetically pleasing
websites. There are component libraries like <a href="https://flowbite.com/">Flowbite</a>
which let you add fancy widgets and doodads galore to your modern Next.js stack
with 10,000 node_module dependencies.</p>
<p>As any experienced web developer will tell you, though, these create other
problems:</p>
<ul>
<li>To have a good user experience, you need to spend a lot of time working on
  your design.</li>
<li>You can't just write content: you need a CMS, which is a separate app. </li>
<li>If you make your site render client-side (the default with React) it will have
  no search engine presence.</li>
<li>You probably need to deploy one instance of your site for every region you
  want to serve. If you're going global, that means at least 7 servers.
  Otherwise you'll experience slow load times in many parts of the world.</li>
<li>Also, your framework inherently adds a lot of overhead to load time.</li>
</ul>
<p>All of this costs time and money that could be spent creating content. </p>
<p>Of course, there is one advantage to your complex, modern site:</p>
<ul>
<li>It looks nice.</li>
</ul>
<p>Most sites that we interact with are either web apps, for
which complex frameworks are <em>often</em> a good choice, or advertisements of some
sort, for which aesthetics matter a lot more than functionality. Unfortunately, 
this gives most of us the impression that we should use heavyweight frameworks 
for <em>all</em> our projects.</p>
<p>I'm here to tell you that this is not the case. Here are some advantages of 
simple sites:</p>
<p><strong>Simple sites give primacy to content and are easy for readers to navigate.</strong> 
PG's blog might look outdated but it's super easy to find what you want. With a
simple design it's trivial to follow 
<a href="https://taylorhieber.co/hierarchy-of-information-fundamentals-of-design/">visual hierarchy principles</a>.</p>
<p><strong>They are simple to write and compose content for.</strong> By using a site builder
such as <a href="https://getpelican.com/">Pelican</a>, <a href="https://gohugo.io/">Hugo</a>, 
or <a href="https://jekyllrb.com/">Jekyll</a>, you can get content up in a fraction of the
time it'd take to do it on a platform like Wordpress. This site is built on
Pelican. If you want to get spicy, you can even use something like 
<a href="https://astro.build/">Astro</a> which lets you integrate JSX components into your
simple website.</p>
<p><strong>They are more optimised for SEO than complex sites.</strong> Simple sites consisting
mostly of text are very easy for search engines to understand, which translates
into higher search rankings for your site. You can also get
addons for most site generators which help optimise your site even further.</p>
<p><strong>They load quickly.</strong> This also helps with your SEO. One way that search
engines judge the quality of content is to look at the time to first response
(when the server replies) and first contentful paint (when content actually
appears). On simple websites these take a fraction of the time than with modern
frameworks.</p>
<p><strong>They're easy to maintain and deploy.</strong> Infrastructure providers like
<a href="https://vercel.com/">Vercel</a> and <a href="https://www.netlify.com/">Netlify</a> make it
easy to deploy a simple site in a matter of seconds. 
Even if you choose to deploy the site yourself, writing the CI/CD logic for a
static site is pretty simple.</p>
<p><strong>They can easily be distributed via CDN.</strong> This means that you can put your
site on over 100 servers globally for less than the cost of a single React app.</p>
<h2>Conclusion</h2>
<p><strong>What we often forget is that modern web frameworks aren't designed to be easy
or optimal for solo developers or freelancers.</strong> They're designed to be scalable 
for large organisations. React originates from Facebook, Angular from Google.
Tellingly, Vue.js, the simplest of the mainstream frameworks, was created by an
individual, not a company.</p>
<p>Next time you're tempted to reach for a heavyweight framework, then, take pause. 
You might be missing an opportunity to make your life much simpler.</p>
